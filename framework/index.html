Framework

A framework for self-directed learning: ad-hoc interests; learning projects; record keeping; and improving learning itself. While self-directed learning is fluid and often unpredictable, a framework aids in rationalizing and in communication with others.

Self-directed learning can quickly take a life of its own. As you uncover previously unknown unknowns, self-directed learning will take you to places you never knew or thought possible.

This is a key strength of self-directed learning, but it can also impede its rationalization and communication.

Here I present a framework that I have found useful for rationalizing self-directed learning and discussing it with others.

Components

Ad-hoc Interests

Ad-hoc interests are the sparks and fuel for self-directed learning. They get you excited about wanting to learn more, and keep you on track in doing so.

Intriguing questions, creative ideas, random thoughts, interesting perspectives and empowering resources—these are all part of your ad-hoc interests.

Flexible Learning Projects

As you pursue an increasing number of ad-hoc interests, organizing them and having a sense of direction becomes important. Guiding yourself through learning projects offers a flexible means of accomplish both the above.

Clean and Lean Record Keeping

Learning occurs in leaps and bounds, and record keeping is crucial in allowing you to pick up from where you left off. As time goes on, record keeping becomes more and more important. Record keeping also facilitates reflection and forms a basis for credibility. Unfortunately, record keeping also comes with its own challenges that the self-directed learner has to overcome.

Improving Learning Itself

The self-directed nature of self-directed learning, in itself, warrants special attention towards improving your learning. Moreover, this is where self-directed learning shines exceptionally.

Inspiration for the Framework

When I first contemplated about taking a year off university to focus on self-directed learning, I was faced with communication issues:

How would I explain my plan to my professors and the administration? How would I explain to them that I was going to be free to pursue whatever I wanted, whenever and however I wanted? These ideas were new to them, even “unheard of”.

To put things into perspective for the university, there was this push within myself to develop a framework: something that would make self-directed learning seem more conceptualized; something that would make it readily acceptable…
