Improving Learning Itself

Rationale

The self-directed nature of self-directed learning is reason enough for special attention to be paid towards improving one’s learning. Improving one’s learning is also an extremely rewarding pursuit, both intrinsically and extrinsically.

The first steps are usually to overcome doubts and misinformation and about self-directed learning. Beyond that, one tries to put into practice other learning improvements that one comes across. The possibilities are unlimited, but here are some of my explorations.

Sense of purpose

Learning and creative work go hand in hand with context and a sense of purpose. I have found the following to give me a sense purpose for self-directed learning:

  • Making the knowledge I gain directly useful in my daily life.

  • Identifying links between different fields, and using knowledge and skills from one field to enhance knowledge and skills in another.

  • Being able to utilize multiple learning resources, and reducing dependence on any one of them.

  • Regaining a sense of dignity and self-trust in my abilities to come up with my own creations that were of value.

  • Being able to continuously build on my discoveries and creations; not trashing them after they were graded.

Gaining new perspectives

We are biased in absorbing and assimilating information

It is a natural tendency for us to preferentially absorb only what we want to hear. We also tend to preferentially assimilate only what fits in with our current world view. The self-directed learner is not immune to this.

Sometimes it is a good thing: such as when we wish to gain trust in our abilities, or when we realise that views initially suppressed were held by others as well. However, it can lead to stagnation and inertia in learning, and in extreme cases make us blind to the truth around us.

Unknown Unknowns

Unknown unknowns are everything that one has never come across or thought of before. Unknown unknowns are distinct from known unknowns:

Let’s say that you are trying very hard to prepare for a spelling test by next week. Though you still don’t know many of the words in the word list, you can still possibly solve your problem. The words in the word list you don’t know how to spell are known unknowns.

But let’s say that because you skipped class, you don’t know that the teacher brought the test forward to this week. Then, unless you get lucky and hear about the change, there is nothing you can do to solve your new problem. It is an unknown unknown.

One can reasonably guess that vast majority of things around us are unknown unknowns. However, due to their nature, we can never quantify how many of them are out there.

Perhaps what we can do is to be humble and try to put ourselves in situations where we are more likely to uncover them. A technique I have found useful is to browse the book shelves in libraries, one by one, and explore titles that catch my attention.

Building habits

Habits are determine our achievements to a large extent. Habits usually determine what we spend a large majority of our time on. They also determine what we do on a consistent basis. It is just so much easier and to do something frequently and continuously if it is a habit than if it is not.

By converting important activities into habits, and breaking by breaking unproductive habits, we greatly improve our our learning. We also make time for what is important and are better able to fulfil our biological and health requirements such as sleep.

Assimilating self-directed learning into daily life

If the activities of self-directed learning become your daily habits, you would not need to put in much effort in learning on your own. You would already do them anyway.

Some general examples are keeping records, visiting the library on your way back home, and carrying writing materials everywhere you go. As you get more experienced, you could form other more personalized habits, such as archiving your records, carrying around a camera, striking conversations with random people, etc..

Sleep

Sufficient sleep is a health necessity. It also allows one to think clearly, spontaneously generate ideas, and be more observant and perceptive of one’s surroundings. Part of improving one’s learning is to get sufficient sleep, at regular times of the day. Unfortunately, we forget about it’s importance, relegating it to

My personal experience has been that sleep has to be explicitly factored into the day’s plan. To get to bed by a certain time, I also need a buffer period of about an hour to finish what I was previously working on.

It also takes considerable discipline to tell myself to put a hold on what I am doing and go to sleep instead. I am usually much more productive when I continue on my work in the morning than if I push myself past my bedtime.

Making time

Making time involves three things.

One, is to determine what is really important to you or what you really wish to do over a certain time period.

Two, is to plan these activities, blocking out times periods for biological activities such as sleep and meal times. It is best to include a buffer period of time between activities to have freedom in dragging them a bit longer if need be.

Three is to say no the activities and people that you could not accommodate into your schedule, without feeling guilty about it.

As a enhancement, you could, four, record the other things that you wanted to do by could not fit into your schedule. That will soothe your nerves that you will attend to them at a later time if you so wish to. This is also one of the methods for managing divergence.
