Salary Negotiations

  1. Inspiration

      It looks like the most important things in salary negotiations are information and preparation.

      I’m taking the perspective that it is possible to ask for any salary (or salary increase), as long as it is within the budget of the employer, or if not within the budget, within the limits of additional funds or sources of funding that can be appropriated.

      One could pick any number and substantiate it with one or more lines of reasoning, as long as it is as objective as possible, and supported by facts and relevant data.

      Perhaps it is very much like constructing an argument (claim + reasoning), but it also involves the human aspects greatly as well.

    2. Comparative Employment Offers Track
    3. Value of the Work I Have Done Track

        Let my supervisor give a value for how much I should be paid for the work that I have done. Ask him/her to pay me what she thinks I am worth. If she does not have the budget, substitute the reduced payment for other perks.

      1. Possible Perks
        1. What do I want?
      2. Question to supervisor
        1. What do you think I am worth?
      3. Comments

          This might be a good methodology, as I do not throw out a number at the beginning. I also let my supervisor speak as much as possible, and understand her side of the story, before telling her mine. There is also a chance that my supervisor may put forward a number of my worth that is greater than what I expected him/her to. It would be less adversarial as well. Also, if I do get a higher salary offer elsewhere, it is likely there would be no hard feelings, as I would be maximizing returns to both myself and to the other party as well.

  5. Salary Negotiation Framework
    1. Gratuity payments in NUS to foreigners

        Include that into calculation

    2. Additional payment to foreigners in-lieu of employer CPF component

        Include differential into calculation

    3. Employment Pass requirements

        Minimum Basic Salary is fixed at $3000. This is the minimum baseline from which other criteria are to be taken into account. Probably keep this in case negotiations turn to something like “I can pay Bachelor’s Degree holders less than $3000”. You can bring in frames such as “illegal” if really necessary. But this is of course at the extreme. It is much more productive to negotiate in terms of other factors, perhaps talent or productivity.

    4. Bonus payments in-lieu

        E.g. Additional renumeration for my performance.

  6. Mindset
    1. Strong objective data and information to support my case

        I have to give my manager sufficient information to substantiate her decision to her supervisors and to the university.

    2. Listen to concerns of my manager

        While my manger may wish to pay me more, she would be be limited by factors such as funding, differential of salary between her other staff/employees and skill at substantiating the salary to her colleagues and supervisors.

  7. Questions
    1. What are the frames I should adopt during negotiations?

        What is the overarching theme or concept I should put across during the negotiations? Different frames for different situations?

      1. Possible frames
        1. This is not a world class lab

            I don’t like this frame as it is negative, but it is a start.

        2. Long term career

            This is probably not a good frame, as I’m not looking for this with my current manager.

    2. What is the underlying personality of the manager?
    3. What are the salary limits?

        For example, it may be difficult to justify paying me more than a PhD holder. However, I could turn that around and ask why is there such a large differential between Assistant Professors and Research Fellows.

    4. What is the figure for my proposed/expected salary?
    5. Can I get other benefits instead, if my salary cannot be at a level that satisfies me?
    6. What is the limit for which budget is currently available?
