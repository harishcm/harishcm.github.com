Salary Negotiation: Perspectives

  1. Perspectives

      Below are perspectives on salary negotiation archived for future reference. Several are readers’ comments to the article “The Only Way to Raise Your Salary” by Fred Kofman. Another is from a review to the book “Linchpin: Are You Indispensable?” by Seth Godin.

    1. Bryan W.
      1. Data Architecture Consultant and Database Contractor

        “It is common (in the US) to hear business owners referred to a “job creators”. With that point of reference, there are constant pleadings to reduce the burden of government laws and regulations and taxes on business – if we do, they say, then businesses will “create jobs”. In truth, as this writer correctly points out, businesses create profit, not jobs. They will create jobs only if those jobs are necessary to create or increase profit. And, of course, business will destroy jobs for exactly the same reason. The value is not in the employment of you or anyone else. Indeed, since your cost (as an employee or as an outside contractor) weighs against profit, lowering your cost is the proper goal of a “rational employer”. If I, the rational employer, can obtain increased productivity from you, the employee, at your current pay rate, why am I motivated to raise your pay (and thus reduce the value contribution of your increased productivity)? Rationally, I would thank you for your added value but resist paying you commensurately. The key here for you, the employee, is to operate as if every action you take is part of the negotiation – a negotiation focused not on value but on cost. Your real or potential employer will negotiate toward lowering the cost you impose on their business by your presence. You will negotiate toward the cost the business will bear if your are separated from the business.”

    2. Paolo G.
      1. Protective Coatings Regional Manager Central and Southern Europe at International Farbenwerke GmbH

        “The article reflects how things should be, but things are not always like that. Mr Kofman quotes at a certain point a great discovery in economy, i.e.: “value” is not an attribute of things per se but, rather, an assessment made by the person for whom the thing appears to be valuable. That is exactly the point. What counts is not the value that the employee delivers to the employer, but the value perceived by the employer. The perception is often very fuzzy, and obviously the performance indicators can just give a support not the entire picture. Concentrating on perceived value rather than on actual value delivered changes obviously very much the priority scale for the employee, and is one of the key reasons why many successful managers change often their jobs: the shorter is the period, the higher is the likely difference between perceived and actual value, and the bigger the manipulation options are available to the employee - the most common of which is the development of short term “quick win” policies, cost-cut oriented, that deliver strong results in the short term and weaken the infrastructure. The prevalence of the perceived value is also one of the reasons why so many overpaid incompetent CEOs are on top of important companies. This seems to be a difficult problem to solve anyway, considering how greedy the investors are and the level of pressure experienced by most of the stock listed groups. The consultancy firms should specialized in the assessment of the skills of the managers should probably work on tools to assess not just the standard skills (like target orientation, decision-making attitudes etc) but as well on longer term oriented profile characteristics like capability to pursue long term objectives, cultural flexibility, adaptatability, communication skills at 360 degrees - i.e. with all levels of the organization, from the very bottom to the very top, consistency, etc. Finally, the salary decisions should not be just in the hands of your own boss - but this is a different topic.”

    3. Dr Jack D.
      1. Versatile Writer/Editor Educational Text Projects; Nonfiction/Fiction/Poetry; Seasoned Affordable Speaking Coach

        “I think we’ve moved a bit fast here in dismissing the idea of labor as value. For nothing of this economic scenario would be happening without the labor that built (for ex.) the computers/”valuable objects” on both sides. Neither proprietor would want, have, use, buy or sell one without a fundamental sense of its value—i.e., the further potential it can realize for him/her AS a powerful object created by labor, by somebody’s fundamental work and toil. So in my case as a Profit Mule (meaning an Adjunct college professor), the proprietor can de-value my labor even though it makes the whole school function in the first place: kids pay for my learning, not for the school’s swell boss. This is based on the threat of getting somebody else “about” as effective as I am at building school results and reputation (and that’s another layer of profiteering, churning out more and more teachers when there are scarcely any jobs worth having). This de-valuing is really possible only because we’re unprotected by full-time and/or tenure-track status—you notice that those college profs make a very nice living. All this, finally, is why Adjuncts are beginning to organize the value of their labor (its effective power to create results in the world) into unions. The value of the “thing” they create (education) is real and a multiplier of both student and school potential and value. Oy, unions? Well, what other mechanism is there to force these pure profiteers to pay us more than even our present minimum wage? They are contemptuous of every other approach. We’re looking forward to the struggle—because the profiteers have so blatantly over-extended themselves. We’re the majority of faculty now. We’re the ones without whom the schools cannot function. Should be quite a wake-up call.”

    4. Aaron H.
      1. Due Diligence Research

        “When I started my own business, I realized that the best way to raise my salary was to identify target customers with the most money to spend. Though this sounds like an obvious strategy, I didn’t realize it till I was in business for six months. I was so busy creating a new business from scratch I wasn’t paying attention to who my customers were, as I was just happy to have any work at all. Now I’ve been able to target high income groups (think international lawyers in Switzerland) and have been able to boost revenues significantly.”

    5. Luis R.
      1. ALWAYS OPEN TO NEW CHALLENGES

        “Great article, yet somewhat contradictory in itself: true you need to increase the value you contribute in order to be able to ask for more; yet if you can get your computer for free, or for $500 or for $900 instead of $1,000 and you know it could do the same for you, why pay the $1,000 instead of buying new? change can cost but….new computers bring new features (can’t teach old dogs new tricks) and many companies hire fresh blood precisely to mould them better than promoting veterans from within. Hence I’d say you add value to the equation (true) then you become “indispensable” to the equation. Becoming unique, specialized, ahead of the pack in state-of-art knowledge and spearheading initiatives where your knowledge/training becomes indispensable puts you ahead in the negotiation seat with your employer when it’s time to “think aloud” about moving companies. Then let the competition flirt with you and see how your employer will offer the raise before you ask. Complementing the article, is not just about more value, but about a “Unique Value Proposition”, as in Blue Ocean Strategy.”

    6. Barry F.
      1. Director of Quality and Supply Chain Management at NanoH2O, Inc.

        “Von Mises’ classical economics works very well if people can be modeled as automatons, seeking maximum economic value absent any emotion. Human reality is that most job decisions have a large component of emotion - employers are more concerned about organizational fit than pure performance, and employees are concerned about working in a place where they will be respected and work among agreeable colleagues. As I’ve learned from the wisdom of the HRM’s in my network (as well as my own experience), the most common reason good employees choose to leave is not for higher pay, but for greater respect and opportunity. Top employees will usually tolerate slightly sub-par pay if all the other job factors are positive.”

    7. Adam Z.
      1. Instructional Design · Knowledge Management · Research · TESOL

        “Strictly rational economics fail in the real world. An employer may not recognize your extra productivity, they may not even understand what you do (as is often the case in technical fields), they may recognize it but still decide to keep your pay no higher than what they believe you can get elsewhere. Employers chronically underestimate the costs of retraining and the tacit knowledge their employees possess. (a) Grow your network inside and outside of work, (b) diversifying your skills and keep them up-to-date, © do what you do with passion, (d) don’t let yourself get stuck in a situation where you can’t walk away (i.e. with a huge mortgage), and (e) trust and be trusted.”

    14. Michael L.
      1. Procurement law and public policy thought leader

        “Thought provoking but while determining value add to your employer’s customers is difficult to determine and the rational answer, it is too rational and not nearly complex enough to fit the world most of us work in. As some of the other posts have indicated, almost the only value that matters is the value your employer sees, whether the value is logical or not. Moreover, value to an employer in a large organization may have little to do with the organization’s customers but much more to the your value to your employer in supporting her fights within the organization. There are also high transaction costs in finding and replacing employees with others that theoretically could provide better value at less cost as well as less rational factors such as replacing one or more of your direct reports with much less experienced persons that may in turn raise concerns that the employer herself is overqualified. For some employers, a subordinate who better satisfies the boss’s customers is a competitive threat, not someone who warrants a raise. Bottom line to me is that the value your employer thinks you have will determine your ability to get that raise. In the best case adding value for your employer’s customers would be the most important criteria. In most cases it will be ensuring your actions are seen as supporting goals that your employer seeks, making him look good and feel secure.”

    15. James U H.
      1. Manager, Analytics & Projects

        “The old adage, “it’s not what you know, but who you know” is key for obtaining higher roles of responsibilities that command higher salaries. To that end, relationship management is paramount for individual financial growth in the workplace. In a perfect world, the person who knew the most, or did the most work, would earn the most money. Not everyone sells widgets for their employer so it can be difficult to determine how much value an individual can add to the bottom line. As referenced from Dale Carnegie’s book How to Win Friends and Influence People, developing friendships by persuading people to like you can sway them to your way of thinking and provide you that better job when the opportunity comes along. Heck, if you do it well enough, maybe the leader/president/CEO might create a role along with a higher salary just for you. We all know that this does happen more times than we care to admit.”

    16. This is a review to the book “Linchpin: Are You Indispensable?” by Seth Godin
      1. Wow, severe generalizations abound April 9, 2010
      2. BY Rumi

        (This a a re-post of a comment I made on another forum that is not likely to be seen by anyone else. Since it essentially boils down to a review, I’m going to post it here instead of writing another one.). — I’m reading this book now and “losing my mind” trying to see Seth’s point of view. I inhabit the world of the cogs (and I indeed did not plan to end up here and I do not want to turn my brain off at work) so I know for a fact that several of the assumptions that he’s basing his theories on are wrong. I also noted how the book conveniently mentioned the lizard brain as a judgment to foist upon those of us who disagreed with his premise. I will not accept that judgment.

        Not all companies value linchpins and creative thinkers. To some, they are just troublemakers. Also, my job and many other cogs may be *defined* as unskilled, but the reality can far different. Is it as skilled as that of an architect or engineer? Of course not. But to imply that those jobs are the new norm/middle ground and broadly define those of us below that level (at jobs that *used* to be considered professional) as unskilled, stings. And I refuse to wear that label. We live in a world where our university economies have been pumping out masters degree graduates and Ph.Ds doing endless postdocs, while most of our new jobs are in the service sector. It’s my belief that underemployment is mostly a structural problem, not a personal one that points to lack of creativity, initiative, or energy on my part (plus living in MS and being obligated to stay here doesn’t help… but this is another common problem for the cogs; lack of money and responsibilities to family usually prevent them from fleeing to other states or cities where they can practice their talents)

        I’ve only gotten about 1/3 through the book so far. I’m going to hope that his message gets a little easier to swallow or that at least he no longer insults people like myself. The audience of this book is definitely not people like me. I see it as upper middle class/white collar pablum to appeal to people who have little experience with being poor and being stuck being a cog. To me, only people who have never really been smacked down by illogic and outright theft of their ideas, money and future can say things like you’re stuck being a cog because you *won’t* change. (for example, I see students at the university I work at who literally have their parents *steal* their money from their checking accounts and savings, open up credit lines in their names and ruin their credit – sometimes before they ever even get to college.) Changing your life requires at least some measure of resources to do so. Stating that we *all* can do it if we want to…baloney. No such thing as “can’t” may hold true for a percentage of society, but that doesn’t mean that it really doesn’t exist. One-hundred and fifty years ago, try saying “no such thing as can’t” to a slave. Their lot in life changed not from being all “engaged” with their work. It came from the “uppity” ones that fought back, escaped, and the white abolishionists. It came from a president that refused to allow the country to participate in forcing a populous to be property. And later it came from another president who sent armed troops into Mississippi to demand that the state follow the laws of the land.

        I have no problem with the personal message to completely engage yourself in everything that you do. Giving with no expectation of being noticed or rewarded is the only way to go. So, on a personal level, be courageous and give your job and anything you do your full attention and go the extra mile. But the reality is that most people will take advantage of you, your job excellence will redefine the expectations of your position to a new higher standard and you and others will be judged more harshly. Clueless managers will likely not know how valuable you are so they’ll lay you off with equal frequency as all the non-linchpins. (Unless you spend time in self promotion and schmoozing around your company which would seem to be the opposite of being a linchpin to me).

        This review is by a cog, who lives in a world that no matter how creatively you work and how much involvement you try to get in your job, your management will not let you participate because you don’t have the purchased credentials of a masters degree. You will be left out of meetings, your ideas will be ignored, creativity frustrates them because you’re acting uppity, and you have no hope of promotion no matter what you do. This is my reality. I still engage fully with my job even though it bores me to tears. I still try to be creative and think of things we could do better, but I have to find a “higher up” to sponsor them.

        To sum it up, on a personal level, I thought Seth’s ideas were spot on. A no-brainer, but worded in an engaging and positive manner. But equating them to becoming indispensable and less likely to be laid off in the corporate world was a stretch; one I just couldn’t make. There’s an old saying…something like, the graveyard is full of indispensable men. Ultimately, no one is indispensable. The irony is that we are all remarkable snowflakes; beautiful in our individuality, but all utterly replaceable no matter how far up the food chain you rise. For us cogs, that point is driven home very early in life. I also resented what appeared to be an assumption by the book that those stuck in cog jobs were resistant to change, not engaged in their work, or unskilled. Saying that there’s no such thing as “can’t change” is akin to blaming a hostage that they can’t free themselves from their imprisonment. Sure, some people wouldn’t run if you killed the criminal, opened all the doors and laid out a red carpet and flashing neon directional signs. But most folks…just give ‘em a little wiggle room and they will indeed rise to the occasion. Ultimately, there is nothing wrong that we live in a world where we are not all Nelson Mandelas.
