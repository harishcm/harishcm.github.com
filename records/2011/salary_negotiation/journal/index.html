  1. 2014-03-24
    1. Explored Resource
      1. In the Name of Love
        1. BY Miya Tokumitsu
        2. Online Article, Accessed March 2014
        3. URL 1
        4. URL 2
        5. Initial Perception 2014-03-24

            A different take on the “do what you love mantra” which appears to be popular in career advice to students and youngsters today.

            Miya Tokumitsu points out danger zones and ill effects surrounding this mantra, especially to people forced to do unlovable but socially necessary work for a living—they stand to get devalued, dehumanised, and their contributions “banished from the spectrum of consciousness altogether”.

            Tokumitsu also cites examples of devastating effects of the mantra even on work which is intellectually or socially desirable, such as academic careers, fashion, media and the arts—with poorly paid PhD holders and adjunct faculty, and upaid interns forming a large part of their respective workforces. An ideology that workers are “doing it for love” encourages exploitation, even in intellectually and socially desirable work.

            The article also discusses the exclusion of people who need to work for wages from entire professions or industries—effectively stripping these professions and industries of the diversity found in society, and resulting in work being divided along class lines.

            The article ends with pointing out that effects of “do what you love” affects women more than men, and that it is an ideology that is well suited in masking exploitative mechanisms present in capitalism.

            A thought-provoking read which I have am still trying to internalise, especially with regards to the ramifications.

  3. 2011-11-30
    1. Added Possible Plan
      1. Value of the Work I Have Done Track

          Let my supervisor give a value for how much I should be paid for the work that I have done. Ask him/her to pay me what she thinks I am worth. If she does not have the budget, substitute the reduced payment for other perks.

        1. Possible Perks
          1. What do I want?
        2. Question to supervisor
          1. What do you think I am worth?
        3. Comments

            This might be a good methodology, as I do not throw out a number at the beginning. I also let my supervisor speak as much as possible, and understand her side of the story, before telling her mine. There is also a chance that my supervisor may put forward a number of my worth that is greater than what I expected him/her to. It would be less adversarial as well. Also, if I do get a higher salary offer elsewhere, it is likely there would be no hard feelings, as I would be maximizing returns to both myself and to the other party as well.

    3. Thought

        Statistical tables showing mean, median, quartile and wage bands would be useful only for starting salaries, and for setting the minimum bar for salaries. I however, want to to go beyond, and look for data and information on other aspects which determine or influence differences in wages. This information would be useful to negotiate for increased wages, or for wages at the higher ends of the wage band.
