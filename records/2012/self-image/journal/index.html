    1. Explored Resource
      1. The Dark Side of Power Posing: Cape or Kryptonite?
        1. BY Jay Van Bavel
        2. Article, 2013
        3. IN Scientific American MIND Guest Blog
        4. URL
        5. Initial Perception

            The article cites academic papers related to power posing and its effects on one’s personal psychology. The article argues that power posing may not be a cure-all, and it may have a dark side in that it may reinforce negative self-thoughts leading to lower self-confidence, especially in people who are downtrodden. The article noted that while power posing may help, say, students in elite institutions, on whom the initial research was performed, it may possibly have an opposite effect on many other people.

            The article also highlighted that the situational context under which the power poses are enacted may be more important than the power pose itself.

            It would be interesting to explore the papers cited in the article.

  3. 2013-08-03
    1. Reflection

        I recently read a couple of articles by Lauren Rivera on how employers in “elite” professional service industries (defined as law, investment banking and management consulting) choose their future employees. They were

        • “Ivies, Extracurriculars, and Exclusion: Elite employers’ use of educational credentials”, in Research in Social Stratification and Mobility, Volume 29, Pages 71–90, and

        • “Hiring as Cultural Matching: The case of elite professional service firms”, in American Sociological Review, Volume 77, Issue 6, Pages 999–1022.

        In her articles, Lauren Rivera showed evidence that these firms

        • confined the overwhelming majority of recruiting and recruiting efforts to a very small list of universities while neglecting many other equally or more highly-selective universities,

        • used participation in resource-intensive extracurricular activities—that often required significant parental investment—to further narrow the applicant pools, e.g. favouring scaling Kilimanjaro over recreational hiking, or favouring pay-to-play “club” sports such as tennis or crew over more accessible sports such as football or basketball, or penalising applicants who had to engage in paid work or caregiving in dimensions such as “interestingness”, “sociability” and “well-roundedness”, and

        • used judgements or perceptions of “cultural fit” from leisure profiles as a formal criteria in assessing applicants, often ranking it as being more important than performance during case-interviews or having relevant experience.

        Lauren Rivera argued that the above hiring practices resulted in a “social closure” of graduate employment in these “elite” firms to applicants from at least an upper-middle-class background, and that the hiring process could be likened to process of finding friends or romantic partners, where similarity between persons was a strong draw factor, rather than a process in which the most capable applicants were sought after.

        Reading these articles was somehow particularly soothing, and made me feel much better about myself. Perhaps it was due to hearing a alternative scholarly voice against the onslaught of commentary in recent years equating renumeration with skill, talent and intelligence, and asserting that only the “best” and “brightest” joined these “elite” professional service industries—probably in a circular argument to justify their extraordinary renumeration and give credence to their “advice” and statements (e.g. “too big to fail”).

        But of course, feeling better about myself does not mean that I become satisfied with my achievements so far, or with the status quo of the things around me.

        Instead, it leaves open questions as to where and how do I move on from here, to better myself, and more importantly, in what, and why so?
