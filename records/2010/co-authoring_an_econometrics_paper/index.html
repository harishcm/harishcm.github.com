Co-Authoring an Econometric Paper

  1. Inspiration

      Sadu came up to me and asked if I would be interested to co-author an econometrics paper with him. We would probably be publishing it in a tier-three journal.

      It sounded like an interesting idea and I wanted to see how it turned out.

  2. Ideas
    1. Tracking Singapore’s GDP/GNP/GINI/Quality of life over the years

        Why was Singapore still a 3rd world country at independence although it had been an “impregnable fortress” and “the light of the south”.

    2. A calculation of wasted productivity

        Motivation theory seems to suggest that for creative and undeterministic work, a high level of reward degrades performance significantly. For routine, deterministic work however, a higher reward increases productivity.

        There has been much talk about the knowledge economy over the years, and jobs in the knowledge are touted to be more undeterministic and require more creativity. At the same time however, inequality in pay has risen exponentially.

        Coming up with figures and methodologies for calculating the amount of wasted productivity will be socially valuable.

      1. Questions
        1. How would we define productivity?
        2. What are some of the existing definitions?
    3. Oracles of our time: Who can predict better?
      1. Compare prediction ability between different professionals and experts
      2. Information sources
        1. High level published reports
        2. Newspapers
        3. Press reports
      3. Focus
        1. Predictions that are part of day to day work?
        2. Predictions that affect many people subsequently?
      4. Possible conclusions
        1. Professions/professionals who are hopeless at predicting
          1. Is it meaningful to support them?
    4. Simulating structural tendencies of our current monetary infrastructure
      1. Hypothesis
        1. Unfettered growth is a necessity in the current system
      2. Methods
        1. Shock the system and see what happens
      3. Further investigations
        1. Play out scenarios
          1. What if all waste was recycled back into raw materials

              As happens in nature.

        2. Propose changes to current monetary infrastructure
          1. Monetary infrastructure for closing the waste loop
    5. Solving problems of the world today: Changes to the current accounting system

        Although rural people have much land and other natural and livelihood infrastructure and resources, these are not taken into account in current accounting practice. This is one of the reasons for the income gap between rural and urban people.

        For instance, trees could be classified as assets that grow over time. This however requires recognition of the uses and benefits of trees. Resources are only valuable when we know what they can be used for (even if it is aesthetic or spiritual).

      1. Green accounting
      2. Needs much more development however
    6. How much energy can we sustainably consume?
      1. A fact finding mission to facilitate the global energy debate
      2. Outline
        1. Find amount of solar energy reaching different layers
          1. Ground level
          2. Tropospheric layers
          3. Stratospheric layers
        2. Find amount of solar energy absorbed at different layers
        3. Current state of technology to trap the energy
        4. Environmental impact assessment for the various technologies
        5. Recommendations
    7. Saving ourselves from the brink: The use of safety factors in economic policy and management

        In engineering design, safety factors are commonly introduced to compensate for varibility and uncertainty. Sometimes Reliability Analysis is used to deal with uncertainties.

        There are at least 3 types of uncertainty recognised in engineering: inherent uncertainty, statistical uncertainty, and model uncertainty. Additionally, uncertainty can also be categorized as ontological uncertainty (fundamental randomness) or epistemological uncertainty (lack of knowledge).

        In any case, engineering already has the tools and methods for dealing with uncertainty in rational ways, which have proved successful over time. It would be interesting to apply such tools and methods to economics, especially since much data is available.
