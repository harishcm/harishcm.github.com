Stuttering

  1. Background and reflection

      I am a stutterer. Stuttering makes up a significant part of who I am. There are many techniques out there designed to help stutterers.

      I have tried smooth speech before. Unfortunately, I could not cope with the markedly different way of speaking I had to cultivate. I had to always focus on how I spoke, I had to stick to a certain few slangs, and worst of all, I had to change my personality somewhat. It was effective for presentations and formal settings. But for casual and friendly interactions, I had to change so much of what I was, or of how I would behave. Well, the good thing is that I no longer take casual and friendly interactions for granted.

      Sometimes I feel like improving myself, other times I feel that I should accept myself, at other times I feel that others should accept me unconditionally with or without my stuttering.

  3. Issues
    1. What should my ‘default’ emotion be like?
      1. Healthy skepticism

          While I wish to learn from everyone and make everyone around me happy, I should retain a healthy amounts of skepticism regarding the way people approach, interact and judge me.

    2. How should I approach my stuttering?
      1. Domineering approach

          I do not accept fear in any situation. In any situation, I will just go in and manage my stuttering on the spot. I only take the positives and no negatives from any situation.

      2. Acceptance approach

          This is me. Certain people will interact with me in certain ways in certain situations. I should recognise these different interactions and manage them. Of course, I cannot expect the best even for most of the time, but really it doesn’t matter much.

      3. Indifference approach

          Of course I have no problem. I can speak like everyone else. So just speak. Everyone stutters once in a while. So what?

      4. Elitist approach

          I am better than the people around me. Everyone around me is pretty dumb for doing what they are doing. I don’t need to accept the people around me for who they are. So I can’t care about how the people around me feel.

      5. Rights approach

          Just as people have a right to life, I have a right to stutter. Since I have a right to stutter, both others and I have to accept all stutterers when they do stutter.

      6. Speaking slowly and clearly approach

          I heard this on a drama series on television. The protagonist was advising her friend who, at the last minute, wanted to withdraw from a public debate because her nerves got the better of her, that she had already practiced several times what she wanted to say, and all she had to do was to speak slowly and clearly. It stuck a chord with me, as unlike the approaches above, it was non-judgemental, and plain and simple, yet likely to be just as or more effective than the approaches above.

      7. Ask “Why?”, not “How?” approach

          This approach looks into the motivations behind working to address stuttering. It is a lot of hard work to address stuttering, and it is a challenging endeavour, right from the start. To a stutterer, addressing stuttering is an ill-defined problem, rather than a clearly defined one. In other words, addressing stuttering is not simply a problem of learning or practising to “speak normally”. It cannot be approaching by only asking “How?”. One needs to be asking “Why?” first.

          There are at least two reasons for this. Firstly, what is “normal” in speech is itself arbitrary. everyone stutters occasionally and certain intense situations can trigger stuttering even in a non-stutterer. Normal is often a constant interplay between the judgement of others, and standards that one sets for oneself. Unfortunately, normal could be easily misconstructed by both stutterers and non-stutterers as “perfection”. Perfection is too high a standard and an unreasonable expectation for both stutterers and non-stutterers.

          The secondly, and perhaps more importantly, looking at stuttering as a clearly defined problem lends itself to covert stuttering, which is a personal choice, but can also be viewed upon as a form of cover up, i.e. “if you can’t make it, fake it”. To a non-stutterer, covert stuttering may be a problem solved, but to a stutter, it demands continual sidestepping and skirting, and imposes a huge emotional burden of dealing with the uncertainty, lack of control and fear of being “discovered”. It also makes the act of speaking in common everyday situations an intensely emotional experience, each and every time. A problem “solved” thus leads to other, possibly larger, problems.

          Asking and answering “Why?” first, before asking “How?”, can help with establishing useful and fitting goals for the individual, based on the individual’s desires. Answers to “Why?” can also provide a constant positive push to along the long and challenging journey of addressing stuttering, and become in themselves, part of the solution.

      8. Physical intelligence and voluntary stuttering approach

          This approach attempts to address stuttering, from a more holistic point of view—looking at speech as a form of “physical intelligence”, and using voluntary stuttering to establish a norm during conversations. There are three parts to the approach.

        1. To have a sense of elegance and poise with regards to my posture

            But elegance and poise, I mean the kind that is natural and makes one feel comfortable and at ease, not the pompous and pretentious kind. A good posture improves speech, but more holistically, a person with greater physical intelligence should be able to stand, sit, move and speak with greater ease.

        2. To think of my body as a musical instrument, and feel the resonance in different parts of my body

            It is easiest to see the resonance in action when one is humming or singing a tune. One can resonate or talk/sing from different parts of the body, from the abdomen upwards, to the chest, neck, face, nose, and even head (head—e.g. Chinese Opera). For speech, it is usually most comfortable to resonate in the abdomen, and the lower pitch sounds more formal, for both men and women.

        3. To use voluntary stuttering in my interactions with people

            I am not going to be perfect, and aiming for perfection is like chasing fools’ gold. Voluntary stuttering makes the stutterer in control of his/her voluntary stutters, and allows the stutterer to control the non-verbal interactions during these periods of stuttering. Through the voluntary stutters, the stutter can establish a norm for the conversation. In this way, tensions are eased for the stutter, and the other party is aware of the stuttering from the beginning and can respond and relate without confusion.

  5. Techniques
    1. View videos of myself stuttering
    2. Looking into people’s face and eyes when speaking

        I notice that I developed a habit of looking downwards or closing my eyes when stuttering while speaking to people.

        At an interview, I realised that looking directly into the faces and eyes of people I was talking to seemed to make the message get across better. Also, it seemed to prevent individuals I talked to from getting surprised or smirking while I was talking. Most importantly, it made me feel better. All this, while I was blocking, and saying “um” frequently, with long pauses between words.

        This is an interesting observation that I wish to explore further. Perhaps, each individual I was talking to realised that he/she was being looked at as well, and that he/she was being judged for his/her behaviour and responses. When I closed my eyes however, they may have not had this feeling, or may have felt that they could be themselves as no one (including me) was looking at them.

        There is probably quite a lot I can learn regarding this from social-scientific concepts such as “the gaze”—the politics of looking. Experimenting and learning more about eye contact would definitely help.

    3. Deep breathing and relaxing the spine and diaphragm

        Deep breathing exercises that I have found useful were those in which I had let air fill and stretch both my lungs and all my diaphragm all the way to their maximum capacity and then letting the air out. It seems that there is a considerable feeling of relaxation when deep breaths result in the portions of the diaphragm attached to the spine being stretched and relaxed.

    4. Visualisation

        Visualisation is a form of mental rehearsal for subsequent performances, including conversations, discussions and presentations. It has been helpful in the past, especially in the days leading up to a major event. Visualisations seem to also help me set the overarching issues such as the tome and style of delivery, the emotions that I would be feeling, the thoughts I would be having, and the actions and body language that I would be using. Visualisations need focus and concentration in clearing the mind of confusion and occluded thoughts.

  7. Acquaintances
    1. Talking instead of smiling

        I noticed that my coursemates I have not talked to before do not seem to respond well to my smiles. However, when I talk to them, they seem to respond well. Perhaps I should maintain acquaintanceships with chats rather than with smiles. And it appears that I do not need to smile much while talking either. This may actually make my life easier.

    2. Complimenting others

        I still cannot compliment people immediately and wholeheartedly when I feel something is good. I used to not be able to recognise the achievements of others at all. But now I can, and I have started complimenting.

    3. Carrying out social interactions with most girls

        I am overly concerned about what the interactions might eventually lead to. I am also overly concerned about what others may think when they see me in the interaction. I feel that both are unhealthy and unnecessary concerns.

      1. Things I am confused about
        1. What sorts of words to use
        2. What sort of things to talk about
      2. Exceptions
        1. Female friends who are attached to other friends
        2. Girls significantly taller than me
  8. Factors improving my speech
    1. Sleep deprivation

        I seem to stutter less when I am sleep deprived.

      1. Possible implementation

          Transfer favourable aspects of my sleep deprived state to my well-rested state.

    2. Exercise

        I feel more confident when I spend a considerable part of my daily life exercising.

    3. Consciously adopting a relaxed mindset

        This works, but is usually confined to a short time period—from minutes to hours to occationally days. However, building a habit of adopting a relaxed mindset is likely to be a sustainable and successful endeavour.

      1. Possible challenges
        1. Controlling the ‘relaxedness’

            Several times I have ended up being “too relaxed” but the people around me seem to like it, though I felt concerned that I was not treating them well by being too relaxed. Perhaps it is an unwarranted concern.

    2. The King’s Speech
      1. BY The Weinstein Company
      2. Film, 2011
      3. Website
      4. Perception 2011-09-21

          By “Hollywood Standards”, the movie was not dramatic enough, but I felt that it tried to portray a stutterer accurately, while trying to keep the audience entertained at the same time. To me, several scenes were nostalgic, bringing back memories of the times that I too felt that I had to perform something very well. I too had thought of and put in place modifications, alterations and coping strategies to overcome stuttering, and deliver a good performance. I too have had in situations in which I had to deliver a speech or a performance to an audience that shared an emotional bond with me, and it had come out remarkably well. As is usual in many movies, it was a happy ending. The protagonist achieved self-actualisation of his purpose in his speeches to the public at the time when Britain went to war with Germany. On a personal note again, the movie showed a model of overcoming stuttering that I had subscribed to in the past, but one that I wish to move away from. The above model may let one achieve remarkable feats, but is less useful in the more mundane situations and performances of day to day life, in which one only needs to perform adequately, but consistently, and if less preparation is required, the time freed up can be used for other activities.
