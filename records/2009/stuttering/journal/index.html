  6. 2012-02-07
    1. Edited Outline
      1. How should I approach my stuttering?
        1. Ask “Why?”, not “How?” approach

            This approach looks into the motivations behind working to address stuttering. It is a lot of hard work to address stuttering, and it is a challenging endeavour, right from the start. To a stutterer, addressing stuttering is an ill-defined problem, rather than a clearly defined one. In other words, addressing stuttering is not simply a problem of learning or practising to “speak normally”. It cannot be approaching by only asking “How?”. One needs to be asking “Why?” first.

            There are at least two reasons for this. Firstly, what is “normal” in speech is itself arbitrary. everyone stutters occasionally and certain intense situations can trigger stuttering even in a non-stutterer. Normal is often a constant interplay between the judgement of others, and standards that one sets for oneself. Unfortunately, normal could be easily misconstructed by both stutterers and non-stutterers as “perfection”. Perfection is too high a standard and an unreasonable expectation for both stutterers and non-stutterers.

            The secondly, and perhaps more importantly, looking at stuttering as a clearly defined problem lends itself to **covert stuttering**, which is a personal choice, but can also be viewed upon as a form of cover up, i.e. “if you can’t make it, fake it”. To a non-stutterer, covert stuttering may be a problem solved, but to a stutter, it demands continual sidestepping and skirting, and imposes a huge emotional burden of dealing with the uncertainty, lack of control and fear of being “discovered”. It also makes the act of speaking in common everyday situations an intensely emotional experience, each and every time. A problem “solved” thus leads to other, possibly larger, problems.

            Asking and answering “Why?” first, before asking “How?”, can help with establishing useful and fitting goals for the individual, based on the individual’s desires. Answers to “Why?” can also provide a constant positive push to along the long and challenging journey of addressing stuttering, and become in themselves, part of the solution.

        2. Physical intelligence and voluntary stuttering approach

            This approach attempts to address stuttering, from a more holistic point of view, looking at speech as a form of “physical intelligence”, and using voluntary stuttering to establish a norm during conversations. It has three parts:

          1. To have a sense of elegance and poise with regards to my posture

              But elegance and poise, I mean the kind that is natural and makes one feel comfortable and at ease, not the pompous and pretentious kind. A good posture improves speech, but more holistically, a person with greater physical intelligence should be able to stand, sit, move and speak with greater ease.

          2. To think of my body as a musical instrument, and feel the resonance in different parts of my body

              It is easiest to see the resonance in action when one is humming or singing a tune. One can resonate or talk/sing from different parts of the body, from the abdomen upwards, to the chest, neck, face, nose, and even head (head—e.g. Chinese Opera). For speech, it is usually most comfortable to resonate in the abdomen, and the lower pitch sounds more formal, for both men and women.

          3. To use voluntary stuttering in my interactions with people

              I am not going to be perfect, and aiming for perfection is like chasing fools’ gold. Voluntary stuttering makes the stutterer in control of his/her voluntary stutters, and allows the stutterer to control the non-verbal interactions during these periods of stuttering. Through the voluntary stutters, the stutter can establish a norm for the conversation. In this way, tensions are eased for the stutter, and the other party is aware of the stuttering from the beginning and can respond and relate without confusion.

  12. 2011-09-21
    1. Added Techniques
      1. Deep breathing and relaxing the spine and diaphragm

          Deep breathing exercises that I have found useful were those in which I had let air fill and stretch both my lungs and all my diaphragm all the way to their maximum capacity and then letting the air out. It seems that there is a considerable feeling of relaxation when deep breaths result in the portions of the diaphragm attached to the spine being stretched and relaxed.

      2. Visualisation

          Visualisation is a form of mental rehearsal for subsequent performances, including conversations, discussions and presentations. It has been helpful in the past, especially in the days leading up to a major event. Visualisations seem to also help me set the overarching issues such as the tome and style of delivery, the emotions that I would be feeling, the thoughts I would be having, and the actions and body language that I would be using. Visualisations need focus and concentration in clearing the mind of confusion and occluded thoughts.

      1. Added Techniques
        1. Looking into people’s face and eyes when speaking

            I notice that I developed a habit of looking downwards or closing my eyes when stuttering while speaking to people.

            At an interview, I realised that looking directly into the faces and eyes of people I was talking to seemed to make the message get across better. Also, it seemed to prevent individuals I talked to from getting surprised or smirking while I was talking. Most importantly, it made me feel better. All this, while I was blocking, and saying “um” frequently, with long pauses between words.

            This is an interesting observation that I wish to explore further. Perhaps, each individual I was talking to realised that he/she was being looked at as well, and that he/she was being judged for his/her behaviour and responses. When I closed my eyes however, they may have not had this feeling, or may have felt that they could be themselves as no one (including me) was looking at them.

            There is probably quite a lot I can learn regarding this from social-scientific concepts such as “the gaze”—the politics of looking. Experimenting and learning more about eye contact would definitely help.

  18. 2011-07-15
    1. Ideas for Improvement
      1. Using self-speech and visualisation as a form of rehearsal for actual speech

          I used to do this during my secondary school days, but mainly for the presentations and for other instances when I had to perform in front of a group of people.

          It would be interesting to self-speak and visualise about my day to day conversations. It would also be a testbed for developing my own methods, styles, tones and patterns of voice and speech.

      2. Removing emotions associated with speaking

          Coming from a perspective of looking at speech as a form of learned and/or learn-able literacy, I am just coming out of stage of poor speech literacy.

          Poor literacy is commonly associated with strong emotions and wild self-esteem fluctuations. These emotions and self-esteem fluctuations are initially specific—to the task that one is poorly literate at—but can spill over to other aspects of life over time. A possible indicator for poor literacy is that one cannot be objective about one’s performance at the task one is poorly literate at.

          Currently I have too many emotions associated with speaking. Coming from a speech literacy perspective, it is a possible indicator of poor speech literacy. It is a good step to remove several of these emotions, so that I can move towards being more objective about my speech skills.

      3. Putting myself into situations requiring frequent social interaction

          Again from a speech literacy point of view, repetition is essential to improve one’s skills at a task. Frequent, ‘real’, speaking situations are required for me to become more literate at speaking.

          This idea can be approached from an Operant Conditioning perspective as well.

          From an Operant Conditioning perspective, it happens that our reaction to external stimuli is strongly influenced, not just by the intensity of the stimuli, but also by the pattern of intervals following which the stimuli is applied. One of the methods of reducing a person’s reactions to external stimuli is to apply the stimuli frequently, giving the person little time to ‘recover’ from his/her previous reaction. As time goes by, the person’s reactions to that stimuli will drop drastically. Conversely, there is likely to be an optimal frequency at which a stimuli will produce the greatest amount of response, summed up over time. If interval times are random, the average and summed up response will be increased as well.

          Frequent speaking interactions will thus reduce my discomfort and emotional responses associated with speaking. Keeping myself away from speaking will perpetuate my high levels of discomfort and emotional responses associated with speaking. From the Operant Conditioning perspective, the greatest discomfort and emotional responses, averaged or summed over time, would correspond to purposefully keeping my number of speaking encounters low or moderate, and being forced into difficult speaking situations occasionally.

      1. Stuttering—when did/does it subside (correlations)?
        1. When I was providing and caring for myself multidimensionally?

            When I was in the USA for a work and travel programme, I was working and living away from my family for 3 months. During this period of time, I was hardly stuttering, and I was providing for myself and cooking my own meals, washing my own clothes, etc.. It is probable that being able to self-determine what I wish to do, how I wish to take

        2. When I was in a society that valued individual viewpoints and individual rights?

            When I was in the USA for a work and travel programme, I was free to say anything I wanted, and in any way that I wanted to. Part of my stuttering definitely has to do with the political climate in Singapore (my home country, a city-state). People who speak up against the government in Singapore have been jailed without trial, fined, or made bankrupt.

        3. When I am not anxious or fearful about possible outcomes

            Yes, when I am not anxious or fearful about possible outcomes, I stutter a lot less. Everyone stutters one in a while, and I think I stutter slightly more than the general population. But when I am not anxious or fearful, I can recover from them easily.

        4. When I am able to speak with the emotions I want to portray

            I am fearful of portraying emotions, especially negative ones, and those or unacceptance. Part of it comes with me not wanting to hurt other people, and trying to understand them. But this really is empathy carried to the other extreme, where I allow people to constantly put me down while being nice to them. Given the research that originated from Professor Axelrod’s experiments into iterated prisonners dilemma, what I am doing is hurting me.

            Furthermore, it is as though my emotions come out eventually, together with the fact that I want to suppress them, and the fact that I am anxious, and have a lowered self-esteem.

            I used to not realise that I was afraid of being judged. It was after hearing from other people that they dislike (or are afraid of) being judged that I started to realise that I am too, afraid of being judged. In the society that I live in, being judged is something that happens all the time, and is even welcomed as the norm or the right thing to do in the job market.

        2. Being simply mediocre

            Oftentimes, I only want to be the best or one of the best. Part of it comes from wanting to be a goodie-goodie and a perfectionist in school. Part of it comes from the constant pushing and high expectations of my father when I was younger.

            This extends into my speech. On one side, I want to either make an unusually excellent impression every time I open my mouth, or not simply not speak at all. At the same time however, these days, I do realise that failures, rejections and asking questions, even dumb questions, are integral and oftentimes useful parts life. It is only in school that we frown over such things.

            The disorder comes from the clash of these two ideals. My conversations and presentations end up with me feeling either very good about them or very lousy about them.

      4. Possible philosophies
        1. Self-worth
        2. Speech literacy
      5. Current plans
        1. Practice letting go

            As a start to improvement, I want to practice letting thing go. If some thing goes wrong, just let it go and do not bother to analyze it too deeply. The next attempt will be a fresh one. The failure does not reflect upon me as a person, or change my self-worth. Conversely, if something goes well, just let go as well. The success does not reflect upon me as a person, or change my self-worth either. This is in preparation for being able to consciously and objectively observe the way I am speaking and to seek improvements from a speech literacy point of view.

        I felt nervous to ask a question yesterday and today at a permaculture talk with about 50 people in the audience. It was good that I actually just went ahead and asked today.

        One of the presenters, Gutier, very observantly noted that I was simply nervous and that I stopped breathing while I was trying to speak.
